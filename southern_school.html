§ 03  ·  The Operating Model

Five non-negotiables.

Drawn from Alpha School's published motivation systems and validated by Bloom's research. Breaking any of them degrades the system.

01

Mastery floor at 90%

No daughter advances until she's demonstrated 90%+ mastery of the current lesson. Rushing past a gap compounds into a wall two months later. Math Academy enforces this natively; other apps require parent enforcement.

02

Adaptive difficulty within ±10%

Each child operates in the productive struggle zone — hard enough to require effort, achievable enough to prevent shutdown. Too easy = boredom. Too hard = frustration.

03

Pomodoro discipline

25 minutes on, 5 off. Each session begins with a stated point goal. Breaks are physical — jumping jacks, a stretch, a lap — never screens.

04

Daily minimums + weekly point goals

Visible, gameable progress. Miss the minimum, lose the day's Southern Bucks. Hit it, earn them. Exceed it, earn bonus.

05

Parent as coach, not teacher

Apps deliver content. Mom enforces minimums, celebrates mastery, redirects distraction. Ask: "What's hard right now?" — never "Let me explain this."

Where the curriculum actually comes from.

This is the part that surprises most parents: Jamie does not teach. The apps deliver content, calibrated and adaptive, in a knowledge graph that updates after every problem. Her job is the human envelope around the apps — read-aloud, writing prompts, workshop activities, and the human warmth that AI can't deliver.

85% — DELIVERED BY APPS

The apps do the teaching.

  • Math: Beast Academy, Math Academy, MobyMax
  • Reading: Audiobooks, MobyMax, Membeam
  • Writing instruction: IEW videos, eGUMPP
  • Science: Khan Academy, Mystery Science kits
  • Grading + feedback: Writing Coach GPT, app accuracy

Jamie does not need to know 7th-grade pre-algebra. The app handles every problem at exactly Charley's level. Adaptivity is the product.

15% — DELIVERED BY JAMIE

Jamie owns the human layer.

  • Read-aloud: 15 min/day with Perry · Story of the World
  • Writing prompts: Pulled from G&B PDF for Perry
  • Workshops: Cooking, fitness, practical skills
  • Discussion: Dinner-table questions from Read-Aloud GPT
  • The motivational frame: Huddle, close, celebrate, redirect

She is the coach, not the curriculum writer. That's why this is sustainable for a non-teacher.

When the day goes sideways.

Real homeschool days don't run perfectly. Here's the playbook for the four common failure modes — none requires panic, all require a calm script.

SCENARIO 01

Perry refuses to start.

Don't lecture. Sit down beside her. Open Beast Academy yourself. Read the comic page out loud. Solve problem #1 together. Hand her the iPad for #2. Friction drops to zero in under five minutes. The hardest moment is the cold start; remove the cold-start cost.

SCENARIO 02

Jacey hits a math wall.

This is good news, not bad. Math Academy detected a gap. Tap the "ask Khanmigo" link in the app — it walks her through the misconception. If she's still stuck after 10 min, mark the lesson and move on; the algorithm will revisit. Never let a wall become a 30-minute meltdown.

SCENARIO 03

Charley says "this is dumb."

Don't argue. Negotiate. "Hit your minimum and I'll hear out a curriculum change for next week." Then on Sunday, actually listen. Adolescent buy-in is a renewable resource — refuse to hand the iPad over for a fight.

SCENARIO 04

Jamie is fried.

Call a "minimum day." Each daughter does only her core minimum (math + reading), one Pomodoro each. 50 minutes total. No writing, no science, no workshop. Reset and resume tomorrow. Operator burnout is the #1 system risk — protect against it ruthlessly.

§ 11  ·  Jamie's phone is the command center

Tap a daughter. See everything.

Jamie should not be tethered to a desk to know what's happening. Her iPhone is the command center — read-only on the go, full edit capability when she's logging the day. The custom dashboard's mobile view is designed for one-handed use during the academic block, while she's making lunch, walking past the living room, or stepping outside.

Oversee from anywhere. Minister in person.

The phone view is for awareness, not control. It frees Jamie to step away from the academic block when each daughter is settled, and to step back in with precision when one needs her. Three real-world scenarios:

SCENARIO · MAKING LUNCH

"How's everyone doing?"

9:30 AM. Jamie is at the stove. She glances at her phone — Perry green, Jacey green, Charley idle six minutes. She walks the 20 feet to Charley's room: "Stuck on something? Want me to grab Khanmigo?" That's ministry — not interruption.

SCENARIO · ERRANDS

"Are they actually doing it?"

Wednesday. Jamie ran to Trader Joe's during the academic block (older daughters can handle it). At checkout she opens the dashboard. Two greens, one yellow. She texts Charley: "You okay? Need a nudge?" Replies "yeah just slow." Continues shopping.

SCENARIO · END OF DAY

"Did everyone hit minimum?"

5 PM. Jamie checks the phone. All three minimums hit, Perry exceeded, Charley barely made it. She taps "Award today's Bucks" — three notifications go out. Bedtime conversation: "Charley, was Math Academy harder today, or were you tired?"

What the phone notifies her about.

Notifications are sparse on purpose. Jamie's phone shouldn't be a beeping leash. The dashboard pushes her only when something genuinely needs her attention — and stays silent the other 98% of the time.

§ 13  ·  The Entrepreneurship Engine

The afternoons are where they catch fire.

Academics in the morning is the floor. Building a real business in the afternoon is the ceiling. AI has collapsed the distance between "I have an idea" and "I have a customer" from years to weeks. A 10-year-old with the right scaffolding can now do what required an MBA and $50K of services twenty years ago — and once she sees a stranger pay her real money for something she built, the world opens.

— THE THESIS —

Don't teach business as a subject. Help them run a business as a curriculum. The lessons are real because the customer is real, the money is real, and the consequence of getting it wrong is real.

01
Real customer
A stranger paying real money. Not a parent buying a lemonade out of pity. The first $20 from someone they don't know rewires their brain.
02
Real friction
A customer says no. A flyer doesn't work. The price was wrong. These are the lessons. AI cannot simulate the gut punch of rejection.
03
Real ownership
She earned it. She decides what to do with it. This is where money stops being an abstraction and becomes a tool with a will behind it.

Concept to first customer · 12 weeks.

A repeatable framework that works for any service business — window washing, dog walking, lemonade stand, baked goods, lawn care. Each phase is two weeks. Each phase has a deliverable. Each phase has an AI co-pilot. The girls don't graduate to phase 4 without proof of phase 3.

Jamie's role: scaffold, not save.

The strongest temptation in this whole section is the wrong instinct: do it for them. The girl whose mom built her flyer doesn't learn to build a flyer. Below, what Jamie does and — more importantly — what she doesn't.

JAMIE DOES
  • ✓ Acts as the back-office: registers a Google Voice number for incoming calls so girls don't share Jamie's.
  • ✓ Supervises door-knocking. Stands at the curb. Doesn't talk.
  • ✓ Is the legal/financial adult — invoices in her name until age 13, custodial bank account at 13+.
  • ✓ Reviews what AI generates before a daughter ships it (logos, copy, prices).
  • ✓ Holds the weekly retro: 15 minutes on Sunday. "What worked? What didn't?"
  • ✓ Celebrates the first $25. Loudly.
JAMIE DOES NOT
  • ✗ Build the website for her.
  • ✗ Knock the door for her.
  • ✗ Set the price for her.
  • ✗ Buy customers from her social network (no pity-buying — kills the lesson).
  • ✗ Save her from a bad pitch — let it go badly. The retro is where it gets fixed.
  • ✗ Pre-empt the rejection. The "no" is the lesson.

When the money gets real.

By Year 2, at least one daughter — likely Charley — is generating recurring revenue. At that point the structure stops being lemonade-stand and starts being a real business. Here's the staircase.

STRUCTURE
WHAT IT MEANS
$0–$200

Earnings live in a labeled envelope. Hand-written ledger. No tax implications. Most lessons happen here — pricing, customer service, repeat business.
$200–$1K

Open a Uniform Transfers to Minors Act account at Fidelity or Schwab — free. Daughter chooses to invest some of her earnings. First exposure to compounding.
$1K–$5K
Custodial Roth IRA
Earned income lets minors fund a Roth IRA up to $7K/yr. Tax-free growth for 50+ years. A 12-year-old who puts $2K in a Roth is funding the rest of her life.
$5K–$25K
Texas LLC + EIN
Jamie files a single-member LLC for the daughter's business (~$300 + Texas filing fee). Real bank account. Real liability separation. Real financial education.
$25K+
Bookkeeper + CPA
If a teenager is consistently making $25K+, hire a part-time bookkeeper (Bench, ~$249/mo) and a CPA at tax time. By high school graduation, she has 5 years of operating history. That's the college essay.
— THE COMPOUNDING UNLOCK —

The first $25 from a stranger is worth more than $25,000 from a parent. Once a daughter has done it once, she will do it again. Once she has done it three times, she cannot unsee the connection between idea, work, and money. That's the unlock you're paying for.

§ 15  ·  Risks & mitigations

What could go wrong.

Three failure modes account for most homeschool collapses. Each is foreseeable, each is addressable, each must be designed against from day one.

Severity: High

Operator burnout

The single highest-frequency failure mode in multi-child homeschooling. Treat the daily-operator role as a part-time job with firm boundaries. No Saturday school. Greg leads a workshop weekly. 12–15 hours/week, hard cap.

Severity: High

Charley adolescent revolt

Pre-design "Charley V2" before age 13. She sets her own weekly minimums within parent ceilings. Relationship shifts from oversight to advisory. Don't wait for the resistance to design the response.

Severity: Medium

Motivation system gaming

Kids will rush problems to inflate points. The 80% accuracy gate prevents this — sessions below threshold don't count. Mom verifies at check-in. Never reward speed over mastery.

Severity: Medium

School-day overload

Adding home-stack work on Tuesday/Thursday is the fastest path to family stress. Protect school days as school-only. Saturday catch-up is voluntary, 30 minutes max, one subject.

Severity: Low

Curriculum mismatch

MAP Growth fall/winter/spring tests catch this objectively. If any daughter isn't tracking 1+ grade level/year on a subject, replace the platform. Evidence-based, not preference-based.

Severity: Low

Texas compliance

Texas is the most permissive state. No registration, no testing, no curriculum approval. Maintain a portfolio anyway — it's the college transcript for Charley in five years.
